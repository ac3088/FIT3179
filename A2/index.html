Natural Disasters in Recent History

Per IFRC, "Disasters are serious disruptions to the functioning of a community that exceed its capacity to cope using its own resources."

Natural disasters are those that are caused by severe weather or climate events, or are in some way related to a natural phenomenon, rather than a man-made one.

Locations and Impacts

When it comes to the human toll of a disaster, Asia and Africa have had it the worst, both in terms of the number of overall people affected, and the death tolls they have endured.

The bar chart shown above displays the 10 countries who had the largest death toll due to natural disasters since 1960. All of the countries in the top 10 are either Asian or African, with the top 3 all being in the South Asia/East Asia region.

The scatterplot shows every recorded natural disaster (in the dataset) since 1960, plotted against the number of people the disaster affected, with each colour representing a different region. Moving the slider to the right will show disasters which affected at least that many of people.

The chart shows that Asia has the majority of high-impact disasters, including all disasters that affected over 100 million people.

Notably, Asia has also endured the most natural disasters out of any region, reporting thousands more disasters than the Americas, more than double the number of disasters in Africa, and over triple the number of reported disasters in Europe.

When it comes to damages/costs, The United States has gotten the short end of the stick, with it having lost more than both China and Japan combined, which are the second and third most affected nations in this case, respectively.

Additionally, the cost of natural disasters seems to be increasing over time, with the total damages each year caused by disasters generally trending upwards.

Types of Disasters

Storms and floods are the most common natural disasters, accounting for over 50% of reported natural disasters since 1960.

Note that these statistics are not reflected in the above map, as locational data for many natural disasters(notably storms) was not available.

Historically, the deadliest type of natural disaster has been droughts. However, more recently, extreme temperatures and earthquakes tend to cause more deaths on average in a given year compared to droughts, albeit the average deaths has dropped significantly.

The bar chart above shows the average number of deaths caused by each type of natural disaster from the given year to 2023. As the year range approaches the present year, earthquakes and extreme temperatures become the most deadly disasters on average, overtaking droughts.

In terms of the distribution of disasters, floods are experienced frequently in most regions of the world, whilst storms mostly hit Asian and American countries.

Interestingly, Africa has experienced by far the most epidemics, surpassing all other regions combined. Asia is in a similar situation when it comes to earthquakes.
